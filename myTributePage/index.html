Singapore Sign Language

History of Singapore Sign Language

  • 1950s - Peng Tsu Ying is credited as a pioneer in establishing Singapore Sign Language.

  • 1966 - Bilingual policy in Singapore came into effect in 1966. Deaf students who had been learning Shanghainese Sign Language (SSL), which is associated with written Chinese have to move towards leaarning English as a medium of instruction.

  • 1970s - An former student of Peng, Lim Chin Heng went to USA to learn ASL.

  • 1974 - Lim returned to Singapore and introduced ASL to the deaf community here.

  • 1976 - SADeaf invited Frances M. Parsons, to promote Total Communication - a philosophy which uses signs, speech, gestures, speech reading, amplification, finger spelling. Lim also brought back SEE-II to the local deaf community.

  • 1977 - Total Communication approach was fully implemented.

  • 1985 - SADeaf joined the World Federation of the Deaf.

  • 2008 - Andrew Tay coined the term Singapore Sign Language (SgSL). Tay emphasised the importance of SgSL in the deaf community to help deaf people build self-esteem, confidence and recognition of their deaf identity.

  • 2017 - Singapore School for the Deaf was shut down due to decrease in enrollment. Students were transferred to Lighthouse School, a special ed school for children who are visually or hearing impaired.

  • 2018 - Deaf students who attend Lighthouse School at primary level before getting mainstreamed at the Secondary Level.

  • Present - Today, SgSL is a continually developing blend of Shanghainese, American and locally generated signs. It comprises of different types of systems such as - SEE-II, Pidgin Signed English and gestures.
